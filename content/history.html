History — The YiJing Through Time

Origins — Oracle Bones and the Shang Dynasty (c. 1600–1046 BCE)

The roots of the YiJing reach back to divination practices of the Shang dynasty, centuries before the text existed in anything like its current form. Shang diviners heated the shoulder bones of oxen or the shells of tortoises until they cracked, then interpreted the crack patterns to answer questions put to ancestral spirits. Tens of thousands of these oracle bones survive, many inscribed with the questions asked and the answers received.

This was a culture in which divination was not a marginal practice but a central instrument of statecraft. No major decision — military, agricultural, ritual — was made without consulting the oracle. The practice was so systematic that a professional class of diviners emerged, and the results were archived. These archives are among the oldest written records in the world.

Alongside the bone oracle, a separate counting-based divination system was developing — one that used tallied numbers to produce symbolic figures. This counting system is the direct ancestor of the hexagram method.

The Three Sages — Fu Xi, King Wen, and the Duke of Zhou (c. 2800–1000 BCE)

The YiJing tradition attributes its creation to three figures across three eras. Modern scholarship views this as a mythological framework for a text that evolved over centuries — but the framework itself is meaningful.

Fu Xi (mythical, c. 2800 BCE)

Fu Xi is the legendary first emperor of China, one of the Three Sovereigns of ancient myth. He is said to have discovered the eight trigrams by contemplating the patterns of nature — the round heaven and square earth, the movements of water and fire, the forms of plants and animals. According to the tradition, he received the trigrams as a revelation rather than invented them — they were the structure of reality itself, made visible.

The eight trigrams he received are the building blocks of the entire hexagram system. Each trigram is a three-line figure made of broken (yin) and unbroken (yang) lines, representing one of eight fundamental forces: Heaven, Earth, Thunder, Water, Mountain, Wind, Fire, Lake. The arrangement of these eight trigrams in two concentric circles — the Earlier Heaven (Fu Xi) arrangement and the Later Heaven (King Wen) arrangement — became central to Chinese cosmology, feng shui, and martial arts.

Earlier Heaven and Later Heaven Ba Gua

The eight trigrams can be arranged in two fundamentally different sequences, each serving a different purpose in the tradition:

The Earlier Heaven arrangement (Xiān Tiān) — attributed to Fu Xi — places the trigrams in a pattern of pure cosmic symmetry. Heaven (Qián) faces Earth (Kūn), Fire (Lí) faces Water (Kǎn), Thunder (Zhèn) faces Wind (Xùn), Mountain (Gèn) faces Lake (Duì). This arrangement describes the natural order of the universe as it exists before human intervention — the eternal, underlying structure of yin and yang in balance. It is used in cosmological mapping, feng shui, and meditation.

The Later Heaven arrangement (Hòu Tiān) — attributed to King Wen — places the trigrams according to their dynamic seasonal and directional relationships as experienced on earth. Thunder in the East (spring), Fire in the South (summer), Lake in the West (autumn), Water in the North (winter). This is the arrangement used for divination and for understanding the YiJing as a guide to navigating change in the human world. It is the arrangement underlying the 64 hexagrams in their King Wen sequence. The Song dynasty scholar Shao Yong produced the definitive diagrams of both arrangements in the 11th century, and they became foundational to all subsequent YiJing study.

King Wen (c. 1112–1050 BCE)

King Wen was the founder of the Zhou dynasty and one of the towering figures of Chinese history. Imprisoned by the last and notoriously cruel Shang emperor, King Wen is said to have spent his captivity meditating on the eight trigrams of Fu Xi and discovering how to combine them in pairs to produce 64 hexagrams. To each hexagram he assigned a name and a brief oracular judgement.

The sequence in which he arranged the 64 hexagrams — the King Wen sequence that remains standard in all traditional editions — has fascinated scholars and mystics ever since. It is not arranged in any simple numerical order, and its logic is debated. Terence McKenna argued it encoded a fractal map of time. Edward Shaughnessy has argued the arrangement reflects the logic of narrative — each hexagram arising from the situation created by its predecessor.

The Duke of Zhou (c. 1040 BCE)

The Duke of Zhou was King Wen's son and one of the greatest statesmen in Chinese history, serving as regent after his father's death. He is credited with adding the line texts — the six-line oracular statements that accompany each hexagram, describing the significance of each individual line. This layer transformed the hexagrams from named symbols with brief judgements into a rich symbolic language capable of addressing the full range of human situations.

Confucius and the Ten Wings (551–479 BCE and after)

Confucius is traditionally credited with the Ten Wings — ten appendices of philosophical commentary that transformed the YiJing from a divination manual into a philosophical classic. According to the Analects, Confucius said: "If some years were added to my life, I would give fifty to the study of the YiJing, and might then escape falling into great errors."

Modern scholarship, including the analysis of the Mawangdui manuscript, suggests the Ten Wings were not written by Confucius himself but by Confucian scholars over several generations following his death — roughly 4th to 3rd century BCE. Interestingly, one of the Mawangdui commentaries quotes Confucius at length on his earlier, negative view of divination — which he later revised, coming to see the YiJing as a profound philosophical tool rather than a superstition.

The Ten Wings elevated the text to the status of a Jing — a sacred classic, one of the Five Classics of Confucian literature. This canonisation gave the text extraordinary authority and ensured its survival and central place in Chinese culture for the next two millennia.

Han Dynasty Canonisation (206 BCE – 220 CE)

During the Han dynasty, the YiJing was established as the first of the Five Classics — the foundational texts of Confucian education. It became required study for anyone seeking government office, which meant that for centuries, every educated person in China had studied the text.

Han scholars produced extensive commentaries and developed systematic approaches to the text's structure — particularly the Image and Number tradition, which analysed the mathematical relationships between hexagrams and their trigram components. This tradition would flower again in the Song dynasty.

Jing Fang and the Cosmic Correlation System (78–37 BCE)

The Han dynasty scholar Jing Fang produced the most systematic attempt in Chinese history to correlate the 64 hexagrams with astronomical phenomena and the lunar calendar. His two major contributions transformed how the YiJing was understood as a cosmological instrument.

The Eight Palaces (Bā Gōng) reorganised the 64 hexagrams into eight groups governed by the eight pure trigrams. Each palace traces a sequence of transformations — one line changing at a time — revealing structural relationships between hexagrams invisible in the King Wen sequence. This system became one of the primary tools of classical Chinese divination and remains in active use today.

The Bìguà — the twelve Sovereign Hexagrams — identified twelve hexagrams that map directly onto the twelve months of the lunar year. Each Sovereign Hexagram represents a specific point in the annual cycle of yin and yang. Hexagram 11 (Tài — Peace) governs the first month of spring when yang is ascendant. Hexagram 12 (Pǐ — Standstill) governs the corresponding point of yin ascendancy. The sequence forms a cosmological clock connecting the oracle directly to celestial observation. This is the historical foundation for the practice of recording moon phase and season alongside every reading.

Jing Fang also developed the Nájia method, which correlates each individual line of every hexagram with specific elements of the Chinese calendar — the Heavenly Stems and Earthly Branches. This embeds the time of casting into the hexagram itself, treating the reading not as a random event but as a moment-specific snapshot of cosmic quality.

The Coin Method — Dating and History

The yarrow stalk method described in the Xìcí Zhuàn is the original casting method, dated to approximately 200 BCE. The coin method — simpler, faster, and portable — was developed during the Three Kingdoms period (220–280 CE) and became widely adopted during the Tang dynasty (618–907 CE). By the Song dynasty it had largely replaced yarrow stalks among ordinary practitioners, though scholars and ritual specialists continued to use the original method.

The two methods differ significantly in probability. The yarrow method produces moving lines less often — reflecting a philosophical position that structural change (Old Yin, line 6) is rarer and weightier than directional change (Old Yang, line 9). The coin method treats all change as equally probable. This difference in probability is not a technical detail — it encodes a different understanding of how change works in the world.

The Mawangdui Discovery (1973)

In 1973, a 2nd century BCE silk manuscript of the YiJing was excavated from a tomb at Mawangdui, China. Buried for over 2,000 years, it is the oldest substantially complete copy of the text ever found. It differs significantly from the received version: the hexagrams appear in a completely different order — a numerical binary sequence rather than the King Wen narrative sequence — and it includes five previously unknown commentaries. One of these commentaries quotes Confucius at length on his earlier, more sceptical view of divination, which he later revised. The discovery transformed scholarly understanding of how the text evolved and how many versions were in circulation in ancient China before canonisation imposed a single authoritative text.

The Song Dynasty Revival (960–1279 CE)

The Song dynasty saw the most creative flowering of YiJing scholarship since the Han. Neo-Confucian philosophers, particularly Zhu Xi (1130–1200), produced commentaries that became the standard interpretive framework for the next seven centuries.

Zhu Xi's approach — emphasising moral self-cultivation and the investigation of principle (li) in all things — shaped how the YiJing was read throughout late imperial China. His commentary accompanied the text in the imperial examinations.

Also in the Song dynasty, the scholar Shao Yong (1011–1077) developed the Plum Blossom method of divination (Méi Huā Yì Shù) — deriving hexagrams from the observation of natural phenomena and numerical patterns in the environment. He also produced influential diagrams of the hexagram arrangements, including a circular arrangement that anticipated the binary number system by seven centuries.

The Leibniz Encounter (1701)

The French Jesuit missionary Joachim Bouvet had been studying Chinese philosophy at the imperial court in Beijing when he entered into correspondence with the German mathematician and philosopher Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz. In 1701, Bouvet sent Leibniz a copy of Shao Yong's circular hexagram diagram. Leibniz, who had independently developed a binary number system in the 1670s, immediately recognised the hexagrams as a binary notation — yin as 0, yang as 1 — and was astonished to find his mathematical discovery anticipated in ancient China.

This connection between the YiJing's binary hexagram structure and Leibniz's binary mathematics is not coincidental or metaphorical. It is a genuine mathematical correspondence. Leibniz's binary system became the foundation of all modern digital computing. The structural kinship between the YiJing and the digital world is one of the most remarkable convergences in intellectual history.

Richard Wilhelm and the Western Transmission (1873–1930)

Richard Wilhelm was a German theologian and missionary who went to China in 1899 and spent the next two decades studying Chinese philosophy under classical scholars, particularly the Confucian teacher Lao Nai-hsüan. He did not convert the Chinese to Christianity — instead, China converted him to a profound appreciation of Chinese thought.

His German translation of the YiJing, published in 1923, is far more than a translation. It is a deep interpretive engagement with the text informed by years of study with living practitioners. His commentary draws on the classical Chinese interpretive tradition in a way no subsequent Western translation has equalled.

Cary F. Baynes translated Wilhelm's German into English in 1950, with a famous foreword by Carl Jung. The Wilhelm/Baynes edition became the defining Western introduction to the text and remained the most widely read English version for the rest of the 20th century. It is one of the two translations included in this application.

James Legge and the Scholarly Tradition (1815–1897)

James Legge was a Scottish sinologist and the first Professor of Chinese at Oxford University. His 1899 translation of the YiJing — published in the Sacred Books of the East series — is the most literal and academically rigorous of the major English versions. Where Wilhelm sought to convey the living philosophical meaning of the text, Legge sought to render its literal sense as precisely as possible.

Legge was also more sceptical of the text's divination function than Wilhelm — he tended to read it as a philosophical and historical document rather than an active oracle. His translation is invaluable for understanding what the text actually says, as distinct from what interpreters have said it means.

The YiJing in the West — 20th Century to Present

The Wilhelm/Baynes edition, published in 1950 with Jung's foreword, introduced the YiJing to a Western audience already primed by the encounter between depth psychology and Eastern thought. The text became important to the Beat generation of the 1950s, the counterculture of the 1960s (John Cage used it to compose music; Philip K. Dick used it to structure novels), and the New Age movement of the 1970s and beyond.

Physicist Fritjof Capra's The Tao of Physics (1975) argued that modern physics had independently arrived at a worldview consistent with the YiJing's vision of a dynamic, interconnected universe. Terence McKenna applied fractal mathematics to the hexagram sequence and produced his controversial Timewave Zero theory.

Academic scholarship continued to deepen with the analysis of newly discovered manuscripts — particularly the Mawangdui silk texts (1973) translated by Edward Shaughnessy — which revealed how much of what Western readers took as the authoritative text was in fact one version among several that had been in circulation in ancient China.

Bradford Hatcher (1949–2020) produced what many consider the most rigorous modern English translation — a word-by-word rendering that strips Confucian and Daoist philosophical overlay to reveal the raw Bronze Age imagery of the original text. Working independently at his Hermetica.info site, Hatcher made both volumes of his translation permanently free. His character-by-character matrix, which gives three English alternatives for each Chinese word, has become an indispensable resource for serious Western students of the text.

Contemporary scholars like Benebell Wen have brought the text back toward its shamanic and ritual origins, resisting the tendency of earlier Western translations to emphasise its Confucian and philosophical dimensions at the expense of its living divinatory practice. The YiJing continues to be a living oracle — consulted daily by millions of people across Asia and around the world.

The YiJing in Eastern and Western Culture

In Chinese and East Asian Culture

The YiJing is inseparable from Chinese civilisation. Its cosmological framework — yin/yang, the five phases (Wu Xing), the eight trigrams — underlies traditional Chinese medicine, feng shui, Chinese astrology, Daoist ritual, martial arts philosophy, and the arts. The text was central to government, military strategy, and personal decision-making for two thousand years.

In Japan, Korea, and Vietnam, the YiJing was adopted alongside Buddhism and Confucianism as a foundational text of educated culture. Korean and Japanese scholars produced their own commentaries and interpretive traditions. The trigrams appear on the South Korean national flag.

In Western Culture

Beyond the Leibniz connection, the YiJing's influence in the West includes: composer John Cage's use of the oracle to generate random compositional decisions, beginning in the 1950s; Philip K. Dick's novel The Man in the High Castle (1962), whose plot was partly generated by consulting the oracle; and its widespread use in the psychotherapy practices of Jungian analysts. The binary structure of the hexagrams has been noted by computer scientists as a genuine precursor to Boolean logic and digital information theory.

Three thousand years of continuous use. Consulted by peasants and emperors, philosophers and physicists, poets and generals. The YiJing outlasts every era that has tried to contain it.
