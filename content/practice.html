A Guide to Working with the YiJing

1.  Purpose

The YiJing — the Book of Changes — is one of the oldest texts in the world. It is a book for consultation, for contemplation and for orientation. Approach it with an open mind that will allow you to recognise the possibilities for action it presents. It does not necessarily offer advice. More often it offers direction or paths of progress through circumstances and situations we face every day.

You may come with no specific idea in mind and cast the oracle to get general guidance, or bring a specific question. Whichever purpose you approach with, the YiJing can offer insight for contemplation and reflection. It operates on three levels simultaneously:

  • As a divination tool — guidance when facing uncertainty, transition, or a decision that reason alone cannot resolve.
  • As a philosophical text — one of the foundational works of Chinese thought, exploring the nature of change, balance, and the relationship between human action and natural order.
  • As a cosmological map — a symbolic system describing the 64 fundamental patterns of change through which all situations move.

The YiJing does not predict a fixed future. It offers a mirror — a way of seeing your situation more clearly, understanding the forces at work, and finding the action (or non-action) most aligned with the nature of the moment.

2.  Framing a Question

The quality of the response you receive is shaped by the quality of the question you bring. The YiJing responds to open, reflective inquiry. It does not respond well to yes/no questions — it describes situations in their complexity, not as outcomes.

Questions that work well

  • What do I need to understand about this situation?
  • What is the nature of this relationship at this time?
  • What should I be aware of as I move forward with this decision?
  • What is being asked of me in this situation?

Questions that work less well

  • Will I get the job? — Too binary, too outcome-focused.
  • Should I do X or Y? — The YiJing will describe the situation, not choose for you.
  • When will things change? — The YiJing describes quality, not calendar time.

Hold your question in mind during the casting. The act of formulating a clear question is itself a contemplative practice.

3.  Reading a Hexagram

A hexagram is composed of six lines, read from bottom to top. Each line is either yang (solid ——) or yin (broken — —), divided into a lower trigram (lines 1–3) and upper trigram (lines 4–6). Read the hexagram text in this order:

  • The Judgement — the central statement. Read it slowly. It often speaks in compressed, oracular language that rewards reflection.
  • The Image — a description from the natural world embodying the hexagram's teaching. Often the most immediately useful section.
  • The Lines — if you have moving lines (values 6 or 9), read those specific line texts. They describe where movement or tension is concentrated.
  • The Relating Hexagram — if moving lines exist, they transform into their opposite, producing a second hexagram showing where the situation is moving.

The translation text is divided into sections. Use the ◀ Prev and Next ▶ buttons at the top of the reading panel to move through each section — Judgement, Image, and the individual line texts. This is easy to miss and contains some of the richest material in the reading.

The Relating Hexagram

When moving lines are present, you receive two hexagrams: the primary (the situation now) and the relating (the situation becoming). Read them together. The primary describes where you are. The relating shows the direction if the current energy runs its course.

A reading with no moving lines is not lesser — it describes stability or stillness, where the present moment contains its own complete teaching.

Do not rush to interpret. Let the hexagram sit with you. Read the text, set it aside, return to it. The YiJing often reveals its meaning over hours or days.

4.  Divination Ethics

The YiJing is a serious tool. A few principles guide respectful practice:

  • Ask once. Casting the same question repeatedly in hope of a different answer is a misuse of the oracle. If a reading is unclear, sit with it longer before asking again.
  • Ask for yourself. The oracle speaks most clearly to the person who holds the question. Casting on behalf of others, or asking about third parties without their knowledge, is generally considered inappropriate.
  • Be honest. The oracle cannot be fooled, but you can fool yourself. A comfortable reading for a bad decision is not good news — it may reflect what you want to see, not what the situation requires.
  • Respect the response. Even a reading you do not understand or do not like is worth sitting with. The discomfort itself is often the message.
  • Use it to think, not to avoid thinking. The oracle opens reflection. It does not replace judgment, responsibility, or action.

5.  The Practice Journal

Keeping a record of your readings over time is one of the most powerful ways to deepen your understanding of the YiJing. A reading journal allows you to:

  • Track how readings manifested in actual experience
  • Notice which hexagrams recur in your life and what they tend to signal
  • Develop your own interpretive vocabulary beyond the translations
  • Build an honest record of how well you read and responded to the oracle over time

For each entry, record: the date, the question (or situation), the hexagram(s) received including moving lines, your initial interpretation, and — after some time has passed — what actually unfolded and how the reading maps onto it.

The gap between your initial reading and what actually happened is where learning lives. Do not erase or revise your initial interpretations. They are honest data.

The YiJing does not tell you what to do. It shows you where you are. What you do with that seeing is yours alone.

6.  The Yarrow Stalk Method

The yarrow stalk method is the oldest form of YiJing divination, described in the Xìcí Zhuàn — the Great Treatise of the Ten Wings — and dated to approximately 200 BCE. Where the coin method takes seconds, the yarrow method takes fifteen to twenty minutes for a full six-line cast. This is not a disadvantage. The time and care required are the point.

Fifty stalks are used. One is set aside before casting begins. Wang Bi, the 3rd century philosopher, explained that the one set-aside stalk represents wú — Non-Being, the Taoist void from which all things arise. The 49 active stalks represent the universe of the diviner.

Why the probability differs from coins

The yarrow method produces a fundamentally different probability distribution from the coin method. This reflects a cosmological understanding of yin and yang:

Line Type Yarrow Coin
6 ⚋× Old Yin (moving) 1/16 2/16
7 ⚊ Young Yang (stable) 5/16 6/16
8 ⚋ Young Yin (stable) 7/16 6/16
9 ⚊○ Old Yang (moving) 3/16 2/16

Old Yang (9) appears three times more often than Old Yin (6). Yang is action — volatile, tending to change. Yin is structure — heavy, resistant to change. The yarrow method honours this: the wind changes direction more easily than the mountain moves. When Old Yin (6) appears in a yarrow reading — probability 1 in 16 — it carries exceptional weight. Something structural is shifting.

7.  Line Positions and Innate Qi

Classical scholarship identifies an interpretive framework for the six line positions beyond the simple yin/yang value of each line — the doctrine of innate qi, the natural energetic character of each position within the hexagram.

  • Lines 1, 3, 5 (odd positions) — innately yang. Active positions of initiative and engagement.
  • Lines 2, 4, 6 (even positions) — innately yin. Receptive positions of response and consolidation.

A line is dignified when its cast value aligns with its innate qi — yang (7 or 9) in an odd position, yin (6 or 8) in an even position. A line is ill-dignified when its value contradicts its position — suggesting tension or misalignment between the moment's energy and what the position calls for.

Line 5 — the ruling line

Line 5 is the position of the ruler — the most authoritative position in the hexagram. A strong, dignified yang line in position 5 is one of the most auspicious configurations in the system. Line 2 is its complement — the minister in the lower trigram. The dynamic between Lines 2 and 5 is one of the most important relationships in hexagram interpretation.

These frameworks are central to the Image and Number tradition and are not taught in Wilhelm or Legge. See the Commentaries section under Benebell Wen.
