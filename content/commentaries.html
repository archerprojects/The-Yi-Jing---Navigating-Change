Commentaries — Thinkers and the YiJing

Relationship to Daoism

The YiJing predates Daoism by centuries, but the two became so deeply intertwined that it is impossible to fully understand either without some appreciation of the other. The Daoist worldview holds that reality is in constant rhythmic motion — the interplay of yin and yang, darkness and light, contraction and expansion, yielding and asserting. This is not conflict but complementarity.

The YiJing is built entirely on this understanding. Its 64 hexagrams are 64 configurations of yin and yang — 64 moments in the endless cycle of change. The Daoist concept of wu wei — non-interference, acting in accord with the nature of things rather than forcing outcomes — is implicit in how the oracle is meant to be used. It does not instruct you to act. It describes the nature of the moment and invites you to align your action (or your stillness) with that nature.

The Five Phases (Wǔ Xíng) and the Trigrams

The Five Phases — Wood, Fire, Earth, Metal, Water — form the second great cosmological framework of Chinese thought alongside yin and yang. Each trigram carries a Phase correspondence, inherited by every hexagram through its component trigrams:

  • Zhèn ☳ (Thunder) and Xùn ☴ (Wind) — Wood. Spring, East, growth, expansion, the liver in Chinese medicine.
  • Lí ☲ (Fire) — Fire. Summer, South, illumination, the heart.
  • Kūn ☷ (Earth) and Gèn ☶ (Mountain) — Earth. Centre and late summer, transition, the spleen.
  • Duì ☱ (Lake) and Qián ☰ (Heaven) — Metal. Autumn, West, contraction, the lungs.
  • Kǎn ☵ (Water) — Water. Winter, North, depth, the kidneys.

These correspondences are the bridge between the YiJing and traditional Chinese medicine, feng shui, Chinese astrology, and the martial arts philosophy of internal energy cultivation. A hexagram is not only a description of a situation — it carries directional, seasonal, elemental, and bodily resonances that the classical tradition considered inseparable from its meaning.

Carl Gustav Jung (1875–1961)

Swiss psychiatrist, founder of analytical psychology. Wrote the famous foreword to the Wilhelm/Baynes English edition (1950) — the single most influential Western introduction to the YiJing. His engagement with the oracle spanned decades and deeply influenced how Western audiences approached it.

  • Synchronicity: Jung introduced the concept of synchronicity — "meaningful coincidence" — as a framework for understanding how the oracle works. Where Western science relies on causality (A causes B), synchronicity describes a different kind of connection: events that are meaningfully related without one causing the other. The oracle does not predict the future; it reads the quality of the present moment.
  • The unconscious and the oracle: Jung used the YiJing with therapy patients and found it remarkably apt across a seemingly infinite range of circumstances. He believed this was because the oracle speaks to the unconscious directly, bypassing the rational ego that so often distorts self-perception.
  • The archetypes: The hexagrams, for Jung, encoded archetypal situations — universal patterns of human experience that recur across cultures and across individual lives. The oracle works because it maps onto these deep structures.
  • The observer is part of the reading: Jung emphasised that the question the person brings shapes the answer they receive. The oracle is not a neutral data source — it is a mirror, and the person consulting it is inseparable from the result.
  • Scientific humility: Coming from a scientific background, Jung was careful not to make supernatural claims. He presented synchronicity as a hypothesis, not a proof — a framework for approaching phenomena that causality cannot fully explain.

Key works: Foreword to The YiJing or Book of Changes (Wilhelm/Baynes, Bollingen, 1950) · Synchronicity: An Acausal Connecting Principle (1952) · The Archetypes and the Collective Unconscious (1959)

Alan Watts (1915–1973)

British-born philosopher and writer, the most influential Western populariser of Asian philosophy in the 20th century. His lectures and books on Zen, Taoism, and the nature of consciousness reached millions. He parted from academic and religious convention by insisting that Eastern wisdom could not be understood through Western rational categories alone — it had to be experienced.

  • The YiJing as a map of the mind: Watts saw the oracle as a model of how the human mind actually thinks — not linearly, but through patterns of relationship, contrast, and change. The hexagrams are not predictions; they are descriptions of cognitive and experiential structures.
  • Yin and yang as the fundamental polarity: Watts argued that the deepest insight in the YiJing is that you cannot have one pole of any polarity without the other. Light requires dark. Self requires other. Gain requires loss. This is not pessimism — it is the structural truth of a universe built from contrast.
  • The oracle as a wise companion: Watts described consulting the YiJing as "having a conversation with a wise old man" who occasionally reminds you not to take things too seriously. He cautioned against over-reliance: do not consult it for every decision, or you will atrophy your own intuition.
  • Wu wei and the oracle: The correct posture for consulting the YiJing is wu wei — non-forcing. You do not extract an answer and implement it. You allow the response to resonate, and you notice what arises. The oracle teaches the art of letting go of the need to control the outcome.
  • The danger of seeking certainty: Watts warned that using the oracle to know the future is a power-kick — an attempt to control what cannot and should not be controlled. The future is precisely the unknown, and its unknownability is what makes it worth living toward. Seeking certainty is seeking to deprive yourself of surprise.

Key works: Tao: The Watercourse Way (posthumous, 1975) · What Is Tao? (lectures, compiled 2000) · The Way of Zen (1957) · Transcript: Reflections on the YiJing (lecture, date unknown)

Benebell Wen (b. 1982)

Taiwanese-American occultist, scholar, and attorney. Author of the most significant new English translation and commentary in decades. Her approach bridges rigorous academic scholarship with living Taoist practice, restoring dimensions of the text that earlier Western translations obscured or omitted entirely.

  • The YiJing as binary code: Wen frames the text as a symbolic representation of the universe built from binary values — yin (0) and yang (1). The 64 hexagrams are 64 six-bit permutations of this binary system, equivalent to 384 lines of code that describe the full range of change in the cosmos. Gottfried Leibniz, who received a copy of the YiJing in 1701, recognised this structure and it informed his development of binary mathematics — the foundation of modern computing.
  • Restoring the shamanic origins: Wen argues that the text's earliest layers are shamanic — oracle bone divination practices from the Shang dynasty. Wilhelm's translation and subsequent Western readings spiritualised the text in a Confucian and later Jungian direction, losing the raw divinatory power of the original. Wen's translation attempts to restore this.
  • The two interpretive traditions: Wen introduces Western readers to the division in classical Chinese YiJing scholarship between the Image and Number Tradition (Xiang Shu Xue), which focuses on the visual structure of the hexagrams and their numerical relationships, and the Meaning and Principle Tradition (Yi Li Xue), which focuses on philosophical and ethical interpretation. Both are valid; most Western readers only know the second.
  • Mathematical synchronicities: Wen traces the numerological architecture of the text — the 64 hexagrams reduce theosophically to 10 (the Heavenly Stems), 64 × 6 = 384 lines reduce to 15 (the Lo Shu magic square sum), and 15 reduces to 6 (the hexagram). These are not coincidences but reflect a deliberate cosmological design embedded in the text from its origin.
  • The YiJing as sacred text: Wen insists on calling it what it is: a Jing — a sacred scripture, in the same category as the Bible, the Qur'an, and Buddhist sutras. Treating it as merely a divination tool or a philosophy text misses its full dimension.
  • The Six Schools of YiJing practice: Wen identifies six distinct approaches to the text in the living tradition: (1) Divination — oracle consultation; (2) Invocation — ritual and ceremonial use; (3) Spell-crafting — talismanic and magical application; (4) Ancient Ways — shamanic and pre-Confucian practice; (5) Humanist-Rationalist — the Confucian philosophical tradition (Wilhelm and Legge both work here); (6) Historical Method — academic and textual scholarship. Most Western readers know only schools 5 and 6.
  • The Four Faces (Sì Xiàng): Between the binary (yin/yang) and the eight trigrams lies an intermediate structure — the Four Faces: Elder Yang, Younger Yang, Elder Yin, Younger Yin. These correspond to the four line types (9, 7, 6, 8). The yarrow stalk method generates these directly through its counting process; the coin method approximates them. Understanding the Four Faces clarifies why moving lines carry different philosophical weight depending on whether they are Old Yang or Old Yin.
  • Shamanic origins: Wen argues that the earliest stratum of the YiJing is shamanic — oracle bone divination practices from the Shang dynasty, predating the Confucian philosophical overlay by centuries. Certain hexagram names and line statements are shamanic formulas. Wilhelm's translation spiritualised and philosophised the text in a direction that, while profound, obscured this raw divinatory power. Wen's translation attempts to restore it.

Key works: YiJing, The Oracle (North Atlantic Books, 2023) · The Tao of Craft (2016) · Holistic Tarot (2015) · YouTube: benebellwen (free lectures and resources)

Terence McKenna (1946–2000)

American ethnobotanist, philosopher, and psychonaut. One of the most provocative thinkers of the late 20th century. Departed radically from academic convention by combining rigorous analysis of ancient texts with psychedelic experience and fractal mathematics. His ideas about the YiJing are speculative and controversial — and illuminating.

  • Novelty Theory (Timewave Zero): McKenna claimed to have discovered a fractal pattern in the King Wen sequence of hexagrams that maps the flow of novelty (complexity and interconnection) through time. By graphing this pattern, he generated a "timewave" that predicted periods of maximum novelty — moments when reality undergoes its most radical transformations. He and Dennis McKenna independently calculated December 21, 2012 as the Timewave Zero point — which also corresponded to the end of the Mayan Long Count calendar. This prediction did not manifest as described.
  • The King Wen sequence as a deliberate construction: McKenna argued that King Wen did not arrange the 64 hexagrams arbitrarily — the sequence encodes a mathematical model of time. Whether or not this is correct, the question itself is legitimate and has engaged serious scholars.
  • The YiJing and DNA: McKenna observed that 64 hexagrams, each made of 6 binary lines, mirrors the 64 codons of DNA — the genetic code. He saw this as evidence of a deep structural principle underlying both biological and cosmological organisation. This parallel was noted independently by several other researchers.
  • Consciousness and the oracle: McKenna understood the YiJing as a map of the psychedelic experience — a topology of consciousness states rather than external circumstances. The hexagrams describe inner landscapes as much as outer situations.
  • Note on Timewave Zero: McKenna's mathematical model was critiqued by scholars including mathematician Matthew Watkins, who found errors in the underlying calculations. McKenna himself described Novelty Theory as a hypothesis, not a proof. His contribution is most valuable as a framework for asking new questions about the text's structure, not as a predictive system.

Key works: The Invisible Landscape: Mind, Hallucinogens, and the YiJing (with Dennis McKenna, 1975, revised 1993) · The Archaic Revival (1991) · Food of the Gods (1992)

Hellmut Wilhelm (1905–1990)

German-American sinologist, son of Richard Wilhelm. World-renowned expert on the YiJing and professor at the University of Washington for decades. Continued and deepened his father's work, showing the text as a living philosophical tradition rather than a fixed historical artifact.

  • Change as the central principle: Hellmut Wilhelm emphasised that the YiJing is not a book of fixed meanings but a system for thinking about change. Every hexagram describes a situation in motion — the question is always about timing, not about static states.
  • The text across Chinese history: His lectures traced how the YiJing was read and applied across Chinese dynasties — as a military strategy manual, a medical text, a political guide, and a cosmological framework. The text's meaning was never fixed; each era brought its own interpretive tradition.
  • The individual within the situation: A recurring theme in Hellmut's work is the relationship between the individual and the larger field of circumstances. The YiJing does not counsel passivity — it teaches the art of reading one's situation accurately and acting in alignment with its actual nature.

Key works: Change: Eight Lectures on the YiJing (1960) · Heaven, Earth and Man in the Book of Changes (1977) · Understanding the YiJing (with Richard Wilhelm, Princeton, 1995)

Edward Shaughnessy (b. 1952)

American sinologist at the University of Chicago, the preeminent Western academic scholar of the YiJing's early history. Departed from received tradition by applying rigorous philological and archaeological methods to the text, producing the first English translation of the Mawangdui manuscript.

  • The Mawangdui manuscript (1973 discovery): A 2nd century BCE silk manuscript of the YiJing was unearthed in a tomb at Mawangdui, China, in 1973. It had been buried for over 2,000 years. The hexagrams appear in a completely different order from the received King Wen sequence, and the text includes five previously unknown commentaries — including one quoting Confucius at length on his changed view of the oracle's importance. Shaughnessy produced the first English translation of this text in 1996.
  • The dating of the text: Shaughnessy dated the core text (the Zhouyi) to the last quarter of the 9th century BCE — during the early reign of King Xuan of Zhou — based on comparison of the language with dated bronze inscriptions. This is more precise and more conservative than traditional dating.
  • The text before canonisation: Shaughnessy's work reveals how the YiJing was used in the centuries before it was canonised — as a practical divination manual consulted for specific military and political decisions, not yet the philosophical classic it became. The text was alive and variable; there was no single authoritative version.

Key works: YiJing: The Classic of Changes (Mawangdui text, Ballantine, 1996) · Unearthing the Changes (Columbia University Press, 2014) · Before Confucius: Studies in the Creation of the Chinese Classics (1997)

Fritjof Capra (b. 1939)

Austrian-American physicist and systems theorist. His 1975 book The Tao of Physics became an international bestseller, arguing that modern physics had independently arrived at insights long expressed in Eastern philosophy — including the YiJing. Capra departed from the strict materialism of mainstream physics to explore the philosophical implications of quantum mechanics and relativity theory.

  • Yin/yang and quantum complementarity: Capra drew explicit parallels between the complementarity of yin and yang in the YiJing and Niels Bohr's principle of complementarity in quantum physics — the idea that particles can exhibit wave-like and particle-like properties, but not both simultaneously. Bohr himself recognised this parallel and chose the yin-yang symbol for his coat of arms when he was knighted.
  • Interconnection and the bootstrap model: The YiJing's worldview — in which all phenomena are interconnected and nothing exists in isolation — mirrors the bootstrap approach in particle physics, where no particle is fundamental and all particles are defined by their relationships to each other.
  • Change as the fundamental reality: Both quantum physics and the YiJing describe reality as fundamentally dynamic — a web of events and processes rather than a collection of fixed things. The hexagrams do not describe substances but situations; quantum mechanics does not describe particles but probability waves.
  • The observer and the observed: In quantum mechanics, the act of measurement affects what is measured. In the YiJing, the question the person brings shapes the answer they receive. Both systems recognise that the observer is not separate from the field they are observing.

Key works: The Tao of Physics (1975, 4th ed. 1999) · The Turning Point (1982) · The Web of Life (1996)

Gottfried Wilhelm Leibniz (1646–1716) — Historical Note

German mathematician and philosopher, co-inventor of calculus, and pioneer of symbolic logic. His encounter with the YiJing in 1701 has profound historical significance — though he is not primarily a commentator on the text, the connection between his work and the oracle is one of the most remarkable intellectual convergences in history.

  • The binary connection: Leibniz had independently developed a binary number system (using only 0 and 1) in the 1670s. In 1701, the French Jesuit missionary Joachim Bouvet sent him a copy of the YiJing's hexagram diagrams. Leibniz immediately recognised them as a binary number system — yin as 0, yang as 1. He was astonished that an ancient Chinese text had anticipated his mathematical discovery by millennia.
  • The foundation of computing: Leibniz's binary system is the direct ancestor of modern digital computing. Every device running on binary code — every computer, smartphone, and digital network — carries a structural kinship with the hexagram system of the YiJing. Benebell Wen's characterisation of the text as "binary code for the universe" is not metaphor — it is mathematical fact.
  • The philosophical implication: If the universe can be described by 64 six-bit binary patterns, and if the same 64-codon structure appears in DNA, and if the same binary mathematics underlies all digital information — the question the YiJing raises is whether this convergence is coincidence or whether it reflects a deep structural truth about reality itself.

For further reading: Benebell Wen, YiJing, The Oracle (2023) — Chapter on Leibniz and binary code · Richard J. Smith, The YiJing: A Biography (Princeton, 2012)

Jing Fang (78–37 BCE) — Cosmic Correlation

Han dynasty musician, mathematician, and YiJing scholar. Developed the most systematic attempt in Chinese history to correlate the 64 hexagrams with astronomical phenomena, the lunar calendar, and seasonal rhythms. His work is the foundation of the cosmic connection tradition — the reason recording moon phase alongside a casting is not a modern innovation but a restoration of classical practice.

  • The Eight Palaces (Bā Gōng): Jing Fang reorganised the 64 hexagrams into eight groups of eight, each palace governed by one of the eight pure trigrams (a trigram doubled — Qián/Qián, Kūn/Kūn, etc.). Each palace traces a sequence of transformations showing how one line changes at a time. This system reveals structural relationships between hexagrams invisible in the King Wen sequence.
  • The Bìguà — Twelve Sovereign Hexagrams: Jing Fang identified twelve hexagrams that map directly onto the twelve months of the lunar year. Each Sovereign Hexagram represents a specific point in the annual cycle of yin and yang — the waxing and waning of cosmic forces through the seasons. Hexagram 11 (Tài — Peace) governs the first month of spring when yang is ascendant. Hexagram 12 (Pǐ — Standstill) governs the corresponding point of yin ascendancy. The complete sequence forms a cosmological clock.
  • The Nájia method: Jing Fang's Nájia system correlates each individual line of every hexagram with specific elements of the Chinese calendar — the Heavenly Stems and Earthly Branches. This allows the time of casting to be read into the hexagram itself. A reading is not merely a snapshot of a situation — it is embedded in the specific quality of the moment, including the lunar phase, the season, and the position of the sun and moon.
  • Why this matters for practice: Recording the moon phase alongside every reading is not superstition — it is the restoration of a 2,000-year-old classical practice. Jing Fang's system treats the cosmos as a living context that shapes the quality of every moment. The oracle reads that quality. The moon phase is part of the data.

Key works: Jing Fang's writings survive in fragments in the Han Shu (History of the Han Dynasty) · Richard J. Smith, Fathoming the Cosmos and Ordering the World (University of Virginia Press, 2008) — most comprehensive Western account of Jing Fang's system · Benebell Wen, YiJing, The Oracle (2023) — covers the Bìguà and lunar correlation in practical detail

Richard J. Smith (b. 1944) — The Panoramic View

George and Nancy Rupp Professor of Humanities and Professor of History at Rice University. Author of the most comprehensive Western history of the YiJing across three millennia. His contribution is not interpretive philosophy but historical scholarship — showing how the text was actually used, argued over, and transformed across Chinese history. He departed from received academic opinion by demonstrating that the cosmological tradition never declined — it persisted and evolved through every dynasty to the present day.

  • The first comprehensive Western history: Smith's Fathoming the Cosmos and Ordering the World (2008) is the first full-length study in any Western language of the YiJing's development from earliest times to the present. Drawing on scholarship in both Asian and Western languages, it covers three thousand years of theory, practice, and cultural application in a single coherent account.
  • Deconstructing false binaries: Smith challenges the sharp distinction between the Image and Number tradition (Xiang Shu Xue — focusing on the mathematical and visual structure of hexagrams) and the Meaning and Principle tradition (Yi Li Xue — focusing on philosophical interpretation). Previous scholars treated these as opposites. Smith shows they have always been overlapping, mutually informing approaches. Most Western readers only know the second, through Wilhelm and Legge.
  • The persistence of correlative cosmology: Contrary to the prevailing academic view that scientific rationalism displaced Chinese cosmology during the Qing dynasty's evidential scholarship movement, Smith demonstrates that correlative thinking — the system of correspondences linking hexagrams to seasons, directions, organs, phases, and cosmic forces — persisted and continues to inform Chinese intellectual life to the present. The YiJing was never merely a historical artifact.
  • The YiJing as cultural biography: His later work The YiJing: A Biography (Princeton, 2012) treats the text as a living cultural object whose meaning shifted across dynasties, social classes, disciplines, and national boundaries. The YiJing that Leibniz encountered in 1701 was not the same text Confucius studied. The text Jung read in 1950 was not the same text Shaughnessy excavated from Mawangdui in 1973. Each era brought its own YiJing.

Key works: Fathoming the Cosmos and Ordering the World: The Yijing (I Ching, or Classic of Changes) and Its Evolution in China (University of Virginia Press, 2008) · The Yijing: A Biography (Princeton University Press, 2012) · Fortune-Tellers and Philosophers: Divination in Traditional Chinese Society (Westview Press, 1991)

The Ten Wings — Commentary by the School of Confucius

Ten appendices to the core YiJing text, traditionally attributed to Confucius (551–479 BCE) but more accurately the work of Confucian scholars over several generations, roughly 4th–3rd century BCE. The Ten Wings transformed the oracle from a divination manual into a philosophical classic.

  • Tuàn Zhuàn (×2) — Commentary on the Judgements: Explains the philosophical meaning of each hexagram's judgement text. One of the earliest and most authoritative interpretive layers.
  • Xiàng Zhuàn (×2) — Commentary on the Images: Interprets the symbolic images of each hexagram and its individual lines in terms of ethical conduct.
  • Xì Cí Zhuàn (×2) — The Great Treatise: The most important of the Ten Wings. A philosophical exposition of the cosmological principles underlying the entire system — the nature of yin and yang, the process of change, and the relationship between the human and the cosmic. Contains the famous statement: "The Changes is a book that cannot be held at a distance." Quoted by Jung in his foreword.
  • Wén Yán Zhuàn — Words on the Text: Extended commentary on the first two hexagrams only (Qián and Kūn), exploring the qualities of pure yang and pure yin in great depth.
  • Shuò Guà Zhuàn — Discussion of the Trigrams: Systematic exposition of the eight trigrams — their natural images, family relationships, cardinal directions, and elemental associations. Essential for understanding how hexagrams are interpreted from their component trigrams.
  • Xù Guà Zhuàn — Sequence of the Hexagrams: Explains the logic of the King Wen sequence — why each hexagram follows the previous one in the order received. Each hexagram is shown to arise naturally from the situation described by its predecessor.
  • Záguà Zhuàn — Miscellaneous Notes: Brief characterisations of each hexagram in a single phrase or sentence — a compressed index to the entire system.

For further reading: Richard Wilhelm, YiJing or Book of Changes (Bollingen, 1950) — Books II and III contain the Ten Wings in full · Hellmut Wilhelm, Heaven, Earth and Man in the Book of Changes (1977)

The YiJing has been a living text for three thousand years. Every generation of readers has brought it new questions — and found that it had something to say.
