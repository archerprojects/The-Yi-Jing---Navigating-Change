晉 Jìn / Progress

above: Lí The Clinging, Flame
below: Kūn The Receptive, Earth

In Jìn we see a prince who secures the tranquility (of the people) presented on that account with numerous horses (by the king), and three times in a day received at interviews.

Overall Meaning

Advance, the symbol of progress

The Tuàn of this hexagram expresses its subject more fully and plainly than that of any of the previous thirty-four. It is about a feudal prince whose services to the country have made him acceptable to his king. The king's favor has been shown to him by gifts and personal attentions such as form more than one ode in the Shī; see especially III,iii. The symbolism of the lines dimly indicates the qualities of such a prince. Jìn means 'to advance'. Hexagrams 46 and 53 agree with this in being called by the names that indicate progress and advance. The advance in Jìn is like that of the sun, 'the shining light, shining more and more to the perfect day.'
